Bank Loan Applications Dashboard

Excel · Pivot Tables · Power Query

14.4K applications $169M funded $183.5M received 84% good loan rate 12.30% avg interest 13.35% avg DTI
Business Problem

Loan performance was tracked, but not in one place

A bank's lending operations team was issuing thousands of loans a month across products like debt consolidation, credit cards, home improvement, and major purchases. Application data existed, but performance metrics — good vs. bad loans, interest rate spread, funding gaps, and borrower risk profile — were not consolidated anywhere a manager could review at a glance.

Without a single reporting view, it was hard to answer basic operational questions quickly: which loan purposes were riskiest, which states had the highest application volume, and whether interest rates were in line with borrower risk (debt-to-income ratio).

Approach

From raw records to a governance-ready dashboard

  1. Collected and validated the dataset. Started with 14.4K loan application records covering loan grade, purpose, term, employee experience, state, and interest rate.
  2. Cleaned inconsistencies. Standardized loan grade categories (A–G), fixed term formatting (36 vs. 60 months), and removed duplicate or incomplete application rows.
  3. Built Pivot Tables to segment performance. Broke down applications by grade, purpose, employee experience bracket, and issuing state to see where volume and risk concentrated.
  4. Used Power Query to calculate KPIs. Computed total funded amount ($169M), total amount received ($183.5M), average interest rate (12.30%), and average debt-to-income ratio (13.35%).
  5. Separated good vs. bad loan cohorts. Classified loans as "good" (current, fully paid) or "bad" (charged off) to isolate the 84% good loan rate from the 16% at-risk segment.
  6. Mapped geographic and term-based trends. Layered in a state-level heat map and a 36-vs-60-month term split to spot regional and structural risk patterns.
  7. Published an interactive dashboard. Added grade and purpose filters so loan officers and managers could slice the report themselves without needing a rebuild each time.
Key Insights

What the numbers showed

84% vs. 16%

Good loans (12.1K applications, $167.2M received) outnumbered bad loans (2.3K applications, $16.2M received) more than 5 to 1.

66% / 34%

Two-thirds of applications were on 36-month terms versus 60-month, suggesting most borrowers preferred shorter repayment windows.

3.5K applications

Borrowers with 10+ years of employment experience submitted the largest single volume of applications — over double the next bracket.

Debt consolidation & credit card

These two purposes made up the largest share of loan applications by far, well ahead of home improvement or major purchases.

852 → 1,736

Monthly loan applications more than doubled from January to December, showing consistent month-over-month growth across the year.

15% / 14% / 12%

Interest rates varied by loan status — current loans carried the highest average rate, ahead of charged-off and fully paid loans.

Recommendations

What the business should do next

  • Investigate the 16% bad loan segment by purpose and term to see if specific loan types (e.g. debt consolidation on 60-month terms) are systematically riskier, and tighten underwriting criteria there.
  • Monitor the rising monthly application trend (852 to 1,736) against staffing and funding capacity to avoid processing bottlenecks as volume grows.
  • Review why current loans carry a higher average interest rate than fully paid ones — this could signal repricing opportunities or early identification of at-risk accounts.
  • Use the state-level concentration data to prioritize regional risk teams in the highest-volume states rather than spreading review capacity evenly.
  • Continue refreshing the dashboard monthly so risk and operations teams can catch shifts in the good-loan rate before they compound.
